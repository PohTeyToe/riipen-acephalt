Hi Nicolas,

I saw the Riipen posting and spent a weekend building something I could point at instead of describe. What follows is a short tour, and a note on where I think I’d be useful.

Everything below is invented. No internal Acephalt material was used. I just wanted to make the conversation more concrete than a resume.

01 Why this caught my attention

One room. Three readers who all think they have legitimate access.

Acephalt’s public framing points at the workflow around investment decisions. The piece that kept pulling on me was the people layer: a single diligence room gets opened by an analyst, a compliance reviewer, and an external counterparty in the same week. Each one has a legitimate, different idea of what they should be allowed to see.

In practice that gets handled by either splitting one document into three different files, or by keeping sensitive material out of the room entirely and emailing it around. Both feel like the kind of friction a platform eventually has to absorb. I wanted to see what it would look like if a room understood roles natively.

02 The problem in one paragraph

The decision about who sees what is doing real work, but it isn’t living anywhere.

A diligence room today is mostly view-only or full-access. That makes the people running the deal pick between oversharing or splitting one artifact into three copies for three audiences. The decision about who sees what ends up living in someone’s head, or in a checklist, or in a series of folder permissions that nobody can audit later. The prototype asks a smaller question: what if that decision lived in one place, at the level of individual fields, and the room rendered itself accordingly?

04 What changes when the role changes

Three roles, not five.

Three is what the diligence pattern actually carries: one party doing the underwriting, one party gatekeeping risk, one party with bilateral information rights. Adding more dilutes the argument.

The shortest honest version of the product argument is this matrix. The same documents stay in one room, but the visible surface changes by role.

05 Why this matters and where I’d be useful

Field-level policy is the smallest believable surface where this becomes visible.

The wider claim under the prototype is small but real. If a platform owns the diligence workflow, the trust boundary inside the room eventually becomes part of the product. Field-level policy is one of the smallest believable surfaces where that becomes visible to a user.

Where I would be useful on the team

  • Turning fuzzy product framing into the smallest believable artifact, and shipping it. This page and the demo are the example.
  • Front-end work that takes a non-trivial data model seriously. The redaction engine here is one pure function and the UI is a strict consumer of it.
  • Writing the explanation alongside the build. The memo route, the captions on this page, and the document audit log are all the same instinct.
  • Working in the space between design, product, and engineering. I do not need a handoff to make a screen real.

06 What I intentionally kept small

Two screens, sample JSON, no backend. I held the line.

A prototype only earns its keep if it tells the truth about its own boundaries. These are the cuts I made on purpose.

No LLM call The memo is hand-authored claim cards bound to the field policy, not generated text.
No real auth The role switcher is a client-side selector that stands in for a server-enforced session role.
No flagging, no comments No upload flow either. Those are different artifacts worth building separately.
No five-role taxonomy No tenancy model. Three roles is enough to make the argument legible.

A production version would push the same predicate down to the database layer. Either a row-level security policy keyed on field and role, or a server-side filter applied before any field set leaves the API. The prototype’s job is to make that future shape feel inevitable, not to be that future shape.
